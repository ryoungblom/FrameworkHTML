Part Three: Risk Analysis Framework

This framework is divided into the aformentioned sections: Legal Risk, Project Transparency, Governance Structure, and Token Functionality

Legal Risk:

This section attempts to predict the risk of a token running into legal issues. The most likely scenario is a token violating U.S. securities law, so here, we will be focusing primarily on the Howey test.

Was there an initial financial investment in new tokens?
ex: are new tokens bought (financial investment), or mined/given out for free/etc (no financial investment)?

Is there a risk of losing value after the initial investment?
ex: Do the tokens provide any **use** within the system? i.e. allowing access, rights, abilities, etc.? If not, they merely exist.

Was there a sale of coins via an ICO or similar?
ex: were coins pre-mined by dev team and then sold at an ICO or similar?

Was there a sale of tokens to fund a pre-existing product?
ex: was there an ICO to develop the project (non-existing), or to fund an existing product (pre-existing)?

After buying the token, does the value of the token depend on the value of the issuing company?
ex: if the token is used in a company's proprietary ecosystem, the token is useless of the company goes under

Does the token do anything other than merely exist?
ex: Do the tokens provide any use within the system? i.e. allowing access, rights, abilities, etc.? If not, they merely exist.

Was there an expectation of profit predominantly from the efforts of a third party?
ex: is the token going to raise in value primarily due to efforts of a third party (i.e. company marketing, building a working product, etc.)?

Project Transparency:

This section attempts to quantify how transparent a project is. This includes things like providing sufficient contact information, information about the founders/board members, and regular status updates.

Does the website provide sufficient contact information?
ex: At this point, things like a slack/telegram, email, facebook, and twitter are non-optional. Bare mininum ("some contact info") would be an email address and one other channel of communication

Is there a community chat system?
ex: Slack, Telegram, or similar

Does the project state a clear goal and use case for its tokens?
ex: "We solve X problem" or "We hope to achieve...", as opposed to a company that releases tokens that don't appear to **do** anything.

Is the project open-source and in a repository?
ex: Project code should be in a public gitHub or similar repository, and should be open source.

Is transparent information easiy obtainable from the website?
ex: Contact info, 'about' page, team profile, github, etc. should all be in a reasonable place on the website. Information that is present but buried in unintuitive places on the site ought to be questioned.

Is there sufficient information about team members?
ex: Important information includes the identities of key team members, founders, board members, and ought to provide information such as previous projects or a linkedin/other website. Bare minimum would be information about the founders.

Does the core team have experience in a relevant sector?
ex: Blockchain, CS/tech, marketing, finance, economics, AI, etc. Be wary of teams with members lacking experience.

Is there two of: a development team, a marketing team, an executive team, and an advisory board?
ex: Solid teams need, at the very least, development and leadership. Complete open-source, decentralized, blockchain-only tokens may only need a dev team .

Are there regularly-provided status updates?
ex: Team should keep token holder appraised of both good news and bad news. Holders should expect regular status updatesâ€”silence is not a good sign.

Is the project meeting benchmarks?
ex: team sets the benchmarks, and should meet them. This includes MVPs and updates, all the way through partnerships and product launches.

Governance Structure:

Here, we look at the structure of the project taken as a whole. The goal here is to make sure that the governance structure is both community-friendly and has mechanism designed to limit the power of the creator(s) or otherwise keep it decentralized.

Is funding released in a controlled manner and measured against benchmarks?
ex: Allocating certain amounts of the funding to specific projects, and only realeasing the next amount of funding when a benchmark is reached.

Were 20%-40% of tokens reserved for the team?
ex: This should include later employees, bug bounties, etc. Ideally, 60+ percent of tokens should be out of the company's control.

Does the project have a decentralized governance structure?
ex: A centralized structure may manifest as a corporation controlling the tokens (and therefor votes), centralized nodes, etc.

Were ICO finances handled via escrow or smart contract?
ex: Companies should never expect that a tokenholder or ICO participant merely send tokens/money/etc to a personal wallet or similar. Ideally, the funds would be held in some kind of escrow or smart contract, to be released upon benchmarks.

Are there provisions for delayed founder liquidity?
ex: This ought to encompass anything that similarly prevents the founders/team members from taking the value generated by an ICO or via token sales and simply walking away from the project.

Was between 20%-40% of tokens offered for sale or otherwise issued?
ex: The founders/core team should never hold so many tokens that the ecosystem is de facto centralized.

Did the ICO have a funding goal, and ended when the goal was met?
ex: Ideally, an ICO should end early if their goal is met, as that marks the end of the funds that they had planned for. Continuing past the funding goal shows a willingness to accept investor money with no real plan for what to do with it.

Is there a hard token cap?
ex: Without a hard token cap, the supply can just keep rising, diluting value while diluting control of the ecosystem.

Token Functionality:

The goal here is to determine the general status of the project. How close is the token to being live? Are the benchmarks and goals set by the team reasonable? Will the token likely be around for a while, as it solves a real-world issue?

Is there a quality, easy-to-find whitepaper?
ex: The whitepaper is the blueprint for the project. No whitepaper, a hard-to-find whitepaper, or a shoddy whitepaper ought to raise red flags.

Is the token "live" and being used?
ex: does the team have a functioning product, or is the product coming sometime in the future?.

Is the token built on a decentralized platform?
ex: Decentralized platforms are less susceptible to manipulation. When the majority of the has power/tokens/other things that have "control" value are in the hands of individuals, the token can be said to be decentralized.

Does the token have utility on the platform?
ex: Does the token provide a right, provide access, do anything other than exist and/or act as a method of exchange between other currencies?

Is the token necessary for the project or for solving a problem?
ex: If the project can exist without the token, the token is more likely to be seen as a security or otherwise regulated. A counterexample is bitcoinâ€”without the token, there is no project.
